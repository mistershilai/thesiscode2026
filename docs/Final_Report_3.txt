1     Introduction

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) has become a major threat to global health. In 2021, an estimated 4.71 mil-

lion deaths were associated with AMR worldwide, including 200,000 in children under five [25]. Sub-Saharan

Africa accounted for nearly 1 million of these deaths [13]. In 2016, the World Health Organization (WHO)

introduced a global action plan to “optimize the use of antimicrobials in human and animal health” and

“develop the economic case for sustainable investment” [8]. Achieving these objectives requires progress on

several fronts: responsible antimicrobial use, research and development of affordable drugs, implementation

of the Global Antimicrobial Resistance and Use Surveillance System (GLASS), and reliable distribution of

medicines through efficient supply chains.

    The Government of Botswana has sought to reflect these priorities in its initial AMR response, with a

draft of the country’s first five-year AMR National Action Plan in 2018 [33]. Although the plan outlined

strategies for surveillance, stewardship, and supply chain strengthening, it was never formally endorsed, and

implementation stalled. A review launched in 2023 now seeks to reactivate these efforts and establish a

sustainable national framework that is awaiting formal approval.

    Even with this renewed commitment, however, Botswana faces persistent operational challenges in en-

suring reliable access to essential medicines. The 2021 Tripartite AMR Country Self-Assessment Survey

(TrACSS) noted that while Botswana has developed basic capacity for antimicrobial management, this ca-

pacity has not yet translated into consistent availability of drugs at the point of care [43]. On 7 March 2022,

average drug availability at Central Medical Stores (CMS), the country’s main procurement and distribution

agency, was 46%, and only 38% for vital medicines [34]. Availability improved to 78% for vital drugs in

2024, but logistical resilience has remained limited. In August 2025, the Government of Botswana declared

a national public health emergency after widespread shortages left clinics without essential medicines, un-


                                                      1
derscoring the continuing fragility of the national pharmaceutical supply chain [36]. The Acting Director of

Primary Health Care, Samuel Kolane, observed in 2024 that better forecasting based on utilization and con-

sumption data is essential to prevent shortages, emphasizing the need for predictive and optimization-based

approaches to public health logistics [35].


1.1    Motivation

Improving antimicrobial availability in Botswana requires strengthening the national pharmaceutical supply

chain. CMS is responsible for procuring and distributing essential medicines to public health facilities across

the country, yet inefficiencies persist in forecasting, lead times, and communication across the distribution

network [23]. These gaps result in uneven access: some hospitals accumulate excess inventory, while many

clinics and health posts frequently experience shortages of first-line antibiotics [23]. Such imbalances interrupt

treatment and accelerate the spread of resistant infections.

   Forecasting and visibility challenges also stem from limited digital infrastructure. In contrast, Tanzania’s

introduction of an electronic logistics system (eLIMS) reduced stockouts from 32% to 23% and long stockouts

from 24% to 15% [24]. Botswana’s Ministry of Health has expressed similar ambitions for data-driven

forecasting, but implementation remains limited, leaving procurement decisions largely reactive. Building

predictive capacity and data-informed planning is essential to improve efficiency, reduce waste, and ensure

equitable distribution.

   Optimizing Botswana’s antimicrobial supply chain therefore presents both a challenge and an opportu-

nity. A network optimization framework informed by real operational data can identify system bottlenecks,

improve stock allocation, and reduce waste from expirations. Such an approach aligns national supply chain

reforms with WHO objectives and strengthens the country’s resilience to AMR-related disruptions.


1.2    Distribution Structure

Botswana’s public sector provides healthcare to roughly 83% of the population and manages large-scale

procurement and distribution of medicines [6]. Since the country does not manufacture human medicines

at scale, the Central Medical Stores (CMS) in Gaborone procures and distributes pharmaceuticals to 27

health districts [42]. These include 3 national referral hospitals, 11 district hospitals, 18 primary hospitals,

222 clinics, 330 health posts, and 931 mobile stops. CMS supplies national referral hospitals and district

health management teams (DHMTs), which operate warehouses and distribute to local facilities. Deliveries

to district-level sites are managed by Botswana Post, a contracted logistics provider.

2    Literature Review

Efficient antimicrobial distribution remains a critical challenge in regions with dispersed populations and

limited healthcare capacity. Although prior work has optimized health supply chains under stochastic as-

sumptions, few network models account for epidemiological feedback between intervention and disease spread.

We aim to embed an SEIR constraint structure into an optimization formulation to dynamically allocate

antimicrobials in response to evolving infection levels. The following section reviews key contributions in

supply chain optimization under uncertainty, robust and adjustable robust optimization, expiration-aware

modeling, antimicrobial resistance, and supply chain resilience.


2.1    Supply Chain Optimization under Uncertainty

Classical supply chain models such as the Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) minimize holding and ordering

costs under constant demand and instantaneous replenishment [12]. Although useful for simple systems,

these formulations do not address perishability, capacity limits, or network constraints that are critical in

health supply chains. Subsequent models incorporated uncertainty to better capture the variability inherent

in real-world systems. Broadly, these models extend deterministic formulations by introducing uncertain

parameters for demand, supply, or transportation times, allowing planners to evaluate system performance

under multiple potential outcomes [45].

robust and adjustable robust optimization methods discussed in the next section.


2.2    Robust and Adjustable Robust Optimization in Inventory Problems

Robust optimization provides a framework for decision making under uncertainty without requiring full

probabilistic information. Instead of relying on expected values, robust formulations seek solutions that

remain feasible for all realizations of uncertain parameters within a prescribed uncertainty set. The general

framework introduced by [3] formalized this approach and demonstrated that tractable robust counterparts

can be derived for large-scale linear programs through duality-based reformulations. Subsequent work by [4]

introduced the budgeted uncertainty set, which limits the number or intensity of parameters that can simul-



                                                               4
taneously deviate to their worst-case values, thereby offering a tunable balance between conservatism and

performance. This approach has proven particularly effective for applications in data-limited environments

such as healthcare logistics, where full distributional information is rarely available.

   Adjustable robust optimization (ARO) extends this framework by allowing certain decisions to adapt

to realized uncertainty. Rather than solving for a single fixed decision, the model defines a decision policy

that updates as new information becomes available. A common approach is to approximate adaptability

through affine decision rules, which express decisions as linear functions of observed uncertainty realizations.

As reviewed by [44], this method enables tractable implementation of adaptive strategies in sequential

production or inventory problems with uncertain demand. Such flexibility is particularly valuable in public

health systems like Botswana’s, where new information on disease prevalence or medicine consumption can

inform mid-horizon allocation updates.


2.3    Modeling Drug Demand

In the pharmacoepidemiology and health services literature, count outcomes such as prescription drug uti-

lization, opioid related emergency department visits, or outpatient encounter frequencies typically exhibit

substantial overdispersion, where the variance of the count variable exceeds its mean. This characteristic

violates the equidispersion assumption of the Poisson model and motivates the use of more flexible spec-

ifications. For example, [37] employs a negative binomial regression to model opioid related emergency

department visit counts adjusted for alternative measures of drug availability, illustrating that negative bi-

nomial models can accommodate the heterogeneity and dispersion that routinely arise in drug related event

data. Their analysis highlights how underlying variability in exposure, case mix, and reporting practices can

generate dispersion levels that exceed the capacity of a Poisson formulation.

   A broader comparison of count data methods is provided by [1], who evaluate Poisson, negative binomial,

zero inflated and hurdle models for outpatient health services utilisation. Their results indicate that zero

inflated models may offer improved fit when the data generating process includes a distinct source of excess

zeros, such as structural non-utilisation at the individual level. Importantly, their discussion clarifies that

the choice between negative binomial and zero inflated specifications depends on whether zeros arise from

the same stochastic mechanism that generates positive counts or from a separate process. This distinction

is central to model selection in applied health systems settings.

   A recent review by [32] similarly emphasizes that drug utilization measures often violate Poisson equidis-

persion and identifies the negative binomial distribution as an appropriate specification in such settings.

The review situates negative binomial models within the broader aims of pharmacological treatment evalu-



                                                       5
ation, noting that accurate characterization of utilization patterns is essential for understanding treatment

effectiveness, resource allocation and potential disparities in access. The discussion also underscores that

variability in utilization arises from clinical, demographic and behavioral factors that naturally produce

count distributions with higher variance than mean.

   Taken together, these studies demonstrate that overdispersion is an inherent feature of drug related

and health service count outcomes, and that negative binomial models provide a flexible and interpretable

framework for addressing this feature. They further clarify that zero inflated approaches are warranted

when a separate zero generating mechanism is present, which provides guidance for selecting an appropriate

model in settings where structural zeros are absent. The zero-inflated structure identified in [1] arises from

structural non-utilisation at the individual level, where a large subpopulation records no outpatient visits at

all. Facility level drug stock data do not necessarily exhibit this mechanism, since all facilities participate in

the supply chain and zeros may represent realized demand changes within the same stochastic process that

generates positive counts.


2.4    Expiration-Aware Modeling

A major challenge in healthcare logistics is product perishability. Antimicrobials, like vaccines or blood

products, have finite shelf lives that impose additional constraints on storage and replenishment policies.

Classical inventory studies such as [26] first formalized the integration of fixed and stochastic shelf-life limits

into inventory theory, laying the foundation for modern perishable goods modeling.

   Subsequent work has treated perishability in various ways. [30] developed a dynamic programming

model that tracks inventory age and discards expired units before replenishment, while [38] introduced

a stochastic mixed-integer formulation for blood products that explicitly manages expiration and rotation.

These detailed models are useful for products requiring strict first-in, first-out rules but can be overly complex

for antimicrobials, which often lack unit-level traceability. Alternatively, [14] used a robust optimization

approach to penalize aggregate waste under worst-case demand scenarios, offering a simpler method for

managing expirations in fast-moving health programs.

   In practice, the shelf life of antimicrobial products varies substantially across classes, ranging from a few

months to several years. This heterogeneity can be incorporated in longer-term simulations of the model,

where drug-specific expiration parameters are used to examine how differential decay rates affect allocation

strategies and waste penalties over time. For Botswana’s context, where batch-level shelf-life data are known

and delivery intervals are predictable, a hybrid approach may be the most appropriate: penalizing expiration

costs while explicitly constraining remaining shelf life. Such a method could capture key operational trade-



                                                        6
offs without excessive model complexity.


2.5    Antimicrobial Resistance and Externalities

Traditional supply chain models rarely internalize the broader public health externalities associated with

antimicrobial use. Delayed or incomplete treatment can accelerate the emergence of resistant pathogens,

creating costs that extend beyond immediate stockouts or expirations [17]. Foundational work by [18]

conceptualized antimicrobial resistance as an accumulating stock in a dynamic system, showing that myopic

prescribing leads to socially suboptimal outcomes. Although these models focus on prescribing rather than

logistics, the principle that short-term allocation decisions have long-term epidemiological consequences

applies directly to supply chain planning.

   Recent literature has attempted to capture these effects through health-based penalty terms or scenario

weighting. Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALYs), for instance, provide a metric for quantifying the social

burden of delayed or incomplete access to essential medicines [7]. Integrating DALY-based penalties into

objective functions allows models to account for downstream health losses, though this approach requires

caution due to limitations in DALY weighting across age and socioeconomic groups [2]. Applying such

metrics to Botswana’s centralized supply chain, albeit with some careful interpretation, could help align

operational efficiency and demand fulfillment with a measure of public health impact.


2.6    Supply Chain Resilience

Beyond efficiency and cost-minimization, resilience, or the system’s capacity to anticipate and recover from

disruptions, is a key consideration in pharmaceutical logistics. [40] showed that pharmaceutical networks are

particularly vulnerable to correlated shocks such as supplier failures, transport delays, and quality recalls,

mainly due to the limited substitutability of essential medicines. Their reliability framework introduced

probabilistic measures of expected shortages and recovery times, which can be adapted to the import-

dependent network of Botswana by treating upstream nodes as suppliers and customs checkpoints rather

than production sites.

   Later work expanded the concept of resilience to include agility and sustainability. [15] proposed the

notion of viable supply chains that maintain continuity in prolonged or overlapping crises. Their approach

highlights that robustness alone may not be enough to ensure adaptability once disruptions occur. In public

health systems with rigid procurement cycles and limited transport capacity, this integration of resilience

and agility is especially important.

   In the context of medical supply chains, unique disruptions arise in the form of epidemics. [10] applied



                                                      7
multi-objective optimization to allocate scarce medical resources under epidemic uncertainty, demonstrating

that resilient strategies must balance efficiency and equity when demand surges occur simultaneously across

regions. Therefore, improved data visibility and communication between CMS and district warehouses

constitute a practical form of resilience, and equitable distribution should be an important consideration of

any accurate predictive model for medical distribution. Further promise in the literature for the integration

of epidemic-based constraints with supply chain networks are discussed in 2.7.


2.7    Embedding Epidemiological Feedback

Conventional supply chain reliability models typically assume that failures and recoveries occur independently

in all locations. In public health systems, this assumption often breaks down because infectious disease

dynamics create correlated and time-dependent demand shocks. Epidemics can synchronize demand surges

across facilities, reduce workforce availability, and strain transport capacity simultaneously. To address

this, several studies have integrated compartmental disease models with logistics and resource allocation

frameworks.

   [5] demonstrated how epidemiological forecasting can directly inform prescriptive decisions by embed-

ding the dynamics of an SEIR-type disease within a data-driven optimization model in the context of the

COVID-19 pandemic. Recent work by [11] combines Bayesian inference with SEIR simulations to gener-

ate probabilistic demand trajectories, showing that uncertainty in disease spread can be incorporated into

real-time decision systems. Earlier theoretical work by [16] extended SEIR frameworks to include treatment-

induced resistance, providing a tractable framework to properly link the extent of medical intervention to

the evolution of resistant strains.

   These models collectively illustrate the value of coupling epidemic evolution with supply allocation. By

linking infection prevalence to demand, planners can anticipate future shortages, prioritize critical regions,

and mitigate the secondary health effects of delayed treatment. For Botswana’s antimicrobial distribution

network, embedding epidemiological feedback would enable the system to adapt to emerging AMR outbreaks

by dynamically updating demand forecasts and optimizing deliveries in response to spatial and temporal

changes in infection levels. Incorporating such feedback structures transforms the model from a static

optimization problem into a responsive decision framework that reflects the interdependence between disease

transmission and supply chain performance.

2.8      Contributions and Gaps

Although extensive research has examined supply chain optimization, perishability, and epidemic modeling,

few studies integrate these dimensions into a unified framework for antimicrobial distribution. This thesis

will address three main gaps in the literature.


    1. Coupling Epidemic Dynamics with Supply Chain Decisions: Most supply chain models assume

        exogenous demand, ignoring how disease transmission shapes real-time needs for medical resources.

        This thesis embeds epidemic dynamics directly into a network optimization framework, allowing infec-

        tions and supply decisions to co-evolve.

    2. Internalizing the Externalities of AMR: Delayed or incomplete antimicrobial access accelerates

        resistance emergence, but current models neglect these social costs. This work incorporates AMR-

        sensitive penalties and DALY-based metrics to quantify downstream health impacts in optimization.

    3. Adapting Reliability Metrics to Import-Based Health Systems: Existing reliability models

        assume internal production networks, which do not reflect Botswana’s import-based pharmaceutical

        system. This thesis adapts those methods to assess resilience across CMS and district-level distribution,

        accounting for disruptions in transport and supply.



3       Data Sources

3.1      Available and Acquired Data

This study draws on several datasets obtained from the Botswana Ministry of Health, as well as former offi-

cials with the Central Medical Stores and at the African Comprehensive HIV/AIDS Partnership (ACHAP),

an important partner in the implementation of this project.


3.1.1     Botswana National Health Facility Data

Facility-level data were obtained from the Botswana Health Facility Master List maintained by the Ministry

of Health and Wellness. The dataset includes the geospatial coordinates, ownership, level of care, and district

affiliations of all registered health facilities in the country. These data were used to identify public-sector

facilities and to construct the network of delivery nodes for the optimization model [21].

    The locations of district warehouses were approximated using the coordinates of each health district’s

administrative capital, or, if possible, the district’s primary hospital. This approach reflects an operational

structure in which District Health Management Team (DHMT) warehouses are typically situated in district


                                                        9
capitals or near the district’s anchoring primary hospital, though the exact coordinates are not publicly

available. These approximations were used to define the intermediate nodes linking the Central Medical

Stores (CMS) to health facilities within each district. A formal data request has been submitted to the

Ministry of Health to obtain verified warehouse coordinates, and the model will be updated accordingly if

an updated data source becomes available [19].


3.1.2   Antimicrobial List

A supplementary dataset of priority antimicrobials was used to identify nationally relevant formulations and

their WHO AWARE classifications. The table lists 67 antimicrobial products and assigns each to one of

three WHO categories: Access (A), Watch (W), or Reserve (R), with additional compounds designated as

non-classified (N/A). This classification enables stratification of drug importance within the optimization

model, ensuring that allocation decisions reflect both therapeutic priority and stewardship objectives [22].

   Shelf-life and storage condition estimates were assigned using representative product labeling informa-

tion from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration’s DailyMed database [41]. This source provides official

manufacturer data on expiration periods and temperature requirements for a wide range of antimicrobial

formulations. To ensure consistency across products, unopened shelf lives were converted to weekly units

based on common pharmaceutical stability ranges observed in hot and humid climates similar to Botswana’s.

Standardized values were temporarily set at 130 weeks for tablets and capsules, 104 weeks for injectable pow-

ders and infusions, 78 weeks for oral suspension powders, and 52 weeks for liquid injectables requiring cold

storage. These estimates reflect conservative, unopened-state stability and will be used to parameterize

expiration penalties in future developments of the optimization model.


3.1.3   Population and Facility Geocoding

Population data were obtained from Statistics Botswana’s 2022 national census, which enumerates all recog-

nized towns, villages, and localities along with their total resident populations. Because the public release of

these data does not include spatial coordinates, each settlement name was geocoded to obtain latitude and

longitude values. Initial coordinates were generated using the OpenStreetMap Nominatim API, combining

settlement and district names in the query string (e.g., “Letlhakane, Central District, Botswana”) to improve

match precision and disambiguate duplicate place names [27].

   To ensure complete spatial coverage and correct potential mismatches, the Nominatim results were subse-

quently validated and refined using the Google Maps Geocoding API, which returned coordinates with higher

positional accuracy for a small subset of unlocated or low-confidence settlements. The combined dataset pro-

duced valid coordinates for 8,321 populated places nationwide, each associated with its corresponding census


                                                      10
populations.


3.1.4    Road-Network Distances and Spatial Linkage

Health-facility coordinates were compiled from the Ministry of Health and Wellness master facility registry

and augmented with warehouse locations. Service-delivery types were standardized into consistent categories

(Health Post, Clinic, Clinic with Maternity, Primary Hospital, District Hospital, and Referral Hospital), with

Clinics and Clinics with Maternity jointly treated as a single clinic-tier level for access modeling. Facilities

labeled as “school clinic” or “prison clinic” were excluded to restrict the analysis to civilian, publicly accessible

sites.

    Each populated census tract was linked to its closest health facilities using a k-d tree nearest-neighbor

search implemented in Python’s scipy.spatial.cKDTree module. For every settlement–facility pair, the

k-d tree identified the nearest facility by minimizing great-circle distance on the Earth’s surface, providing

an initial topological linkage between population demand points and service locations.

    A national routing network was then constructed using the Open Source Routing Machine (OSRM) and

the most recent OpenStreetMap (OSM) extract for Botswana obtained from Geofabrik [9]. The OSRM

backend was deployed locally in a Docker container to ensure reproducibility and full control over routing

parameters [31]. The compiled network, containing approximately 16.5 million nodes and 1.8 million edges,

enabled detailed modeling of travel routes across paved, gravel, and unpaved roads.

    To incorporate transport accessibility, each census tract was routed to its nearest Health Post, Clinic

(including Maternity), and Primary Hospital using the locally hosted OSRM instance. The resulting ori-

gin–destination list provided road-network travel distances (in kilometers) and estimated travel times (in

minutes) between census tracts and facilities. This hybrid k-d tree and OSRM framework yielded a tractable

approximation of service catchments that integrates both spatial proximity and realistic network connectivity.

    The final dataset excluded non-public facilities and retained 10,492 census tracts, each assigned to its

closest health post, clinic, and primary hospital. This integrated approach was able to assign 282 of 328

health posts, 220 of 258 clinics, and 15 of 15 primary hospitals considered in the model. Unassigned health

facilities will be handled on a case-by-case basis to their nearest census tract, and may have resulted from

extremely dense population areas that assigned populations to other health facilities nearby. This spatial

framework will form the basis for subsequent optimization of supply-chain logistics and facility-level resource

allocation [39].

4.2      Notation and Variable Definitions

To construct the first-stage optimization model, we begin by defining our sets, indices, parameters and

decision variables.


4.2.1     Model Structure and Notation

We model the antimicrobial distribution network as a directed graph G = (N, A), where:

   • N : set of nodes representing public-sector health facilities (e.g., the Central Medical Stores, DHMT

        warehouses, hospitals, clinics, and health posts), indexed by n ∈ {0, 1, . . . , F − 1}, with F representing

        the total number of facilities.

   • K: set of antimicrobial product types, indexed by k ∈ {0, 1, . . . , K − 1}

   • A ⊆ N × N : set of directed arcs (i, j) representing feasible transportation routes between facilities;

        flows are permitted only downstream from CMS to DHMT warehouses and lower-level facilities

   • t = {0, 1, . . . , T }: set of discrete time periods representing decision epochs (each typically corresponding

        to two weeks)

4.5.1    Remarks on the Implementation of the MDE

To construct facility level estimates of expected antimicrobial demand, we combine district level demographic

information with empirical evidence on antimicrobial use in Botswana. Our approach begins with census

tabulations disaggregated by age group, which provide the demographic composition of each district. These

data form the basis for estimating the aggregate infection and treatment requirements of the populations

served by public health facilities. Because individual level diagnosis and prescribing records are unavailable,


                                                                20
we assume that age specific patterns of antimicrobial need are approximately homogeneous within each

district. This assumption is reasonable in settings where population mobility is limited and where access to

facilities is determined primarily by geographic proximity.

   To link demographic structure to expected antimicrobial use, we incorporate findings from a 2019 na-

tional point prevalence study of antimicrobial consumption [28]. The point prevalence survey (PPS) was

conducted across hospitals selected to represent rural, urban and semiurban areas, and therefore provides

system-wide estimates of antimicrobial use intensity. However, because the PPS does not necessarily release

facility identifiers nor complete stratified rates for each geographic category, we are unable to isolate hospital

specific prevalence values for rural, urban or semiurban locations. Consequently, we apply a single unified

prevalence rate to all districts and facilities. Implicit in this choice is the assumption that the PPS sample

is representative of national prescribing patterns and that differences in antimicrobial use across rurality

categories do not materially affect aggregate demand projections at the district level.

   Assumptions are necessary to translate population structure into facility level estimates. As alluded

previously, we assume that the age distribution of each district accurately reflects the age distribution of

the catchment populations of facilities within that district. Although facilities may serve subpopulations

that differ somewhat from the district average, fine grained catchment demographics are not available, and

district level age profiles provide the most reliable population based approximation. Second, we treat zeros in

facility level stock or dispensing counts as realizations of the same underlying demand and supply processes

rather than as evidence of an alternative mechanism of structural non-utilization. This assumption supports

the use of negative binomial regression in the subsequent analysis rather than a zero inflated specification.

Third, we assume that PPS derived antimicrobial use prevalence is sufficiently stable over time to serve as

an approximate indicator of treatment intensity in the period for which supply chain data are available.

   Facility level demand is then obtained by allocating district level antimicrobial need to facilities in pro-

portion to their catchment populations. Catchment assignment is determined by geographic proximity and

administrative boundaries, consistent with patterns of health service utilisation in Botswana. The resulting

estimator provides a coherent method for translating population characteristics into expected antimicrobial

requirements, forming an essential input to the negative binomial models used to study stock, consumption

and stockout dynamics in the health system.

5     Preliminary Results

5.1    Deterministic Model Verification on a Toy Network

To verify the internal consistency of the optimization formulation, a simplified single-period toy model

was implemented in cvxpy using the Gurobi solver. The network consisted of two nodes (A and B), one



                                                                       22
pharmaceutical product, and a single directed route from A to B. The formulation included flow-balance,

inventory, storage, vehicle-capacity, and big-M route-activation constraints identical to those used in the full

national model.

    Node A was initialized with 100 units of inventory, while Node B faced a demand of 50 units. The

transport distance was set to 10 km with a per-unit transport cost of v = 2.0, a holding cost of h = 0.5, and

a truck capacity of 100 units. The model minimized total logistics cost, consisting of variable transport and

holding costs, subject to the aforementioned constraints.

    The optimal solution yielded an objective value of 45.0, with a shipment of 50 units from A to B, one

active truck and route, and 50 units remaining in inventory at A. Node B’s demand was fully satisfied, and

all capacity and storage constraints were met exactly. The results confirmed that flow conservation, big-M

activation, and cost-minimization logic were correctly implemented. This test validated the formulation’s

structural soundness prior to scaling to the full multi-period, multi-product national network.


5.2     Simulation Framework

The simulation environment provides a controlled setting for evaluating the behavior of the multi-period

antimicrobial distribution model under both deterministic and stochastic realizations of demand. Because

the national supply chain operates in discrete planning cycles, the simulation proceeds over periods t =

0, 1, . . . , T , where each epoch corresponds to a two-week decision interval. At the beginning of each period,

the model receives updated inventory states, observes or draws the demand for that period, and solves

the corresponding distribution problem. This mirrors the operational structure of CMS and the DHMT

warehouses, which rely on periodic replenishment and routing decisions rather than continuous supply flows.

5.3.1   Expected Admissions Approximation.

Because district-level admission microdata are not available, we estimate age-specific admissions using a

simple hospitalization-rate model. Let P opr,a denote the population in region r and age group a. We define


                                                     25
expected admissions as

                                              Nr,a = ha × P opr,a ,


where ha is the hospitalization rate for age group a. For the present paper, we set ha = 0.05 as a reasonable

working value reflecting the proportion of individuals expected to require inpatient care during a two-week

planning period.

   Once the full national model is applied, we will refine ha using empirically derived hospitalization rates

computed from facility catchment areas. In multi-hospital districts, catchment populations assigned via the

spatial linkage framework will allow us to estimate the average proportion of each catchment that is admitted

per decision period, providing a data-driven update to ha that reflects local utilization patterns.


5.3.2   First Implementation

Demand in each simulation round is generated from the Negative Binomial distribution implied by the NB-

GLM estimator. For facility n in Gaborone, let D̄n denote the mean penicillin demand and σn the dispersion

parameter. In each trial r, realized demand is drawn as

by applying the fixed nominal flows to these stochastic realizations, while the static robust policy uses the

flows obtained from the robust model with Γ = 1.0.

   The ARO-ADR policy augments the robust baseline with an affine correction rule on flows. For each arc

(u, v), the shipment in trial r is given by


                            (r)
                           Fu→v = F̄u→v + βu→v ξv(r−1) ,      ξv(r−1) = Dv(r−1) − D̄v ,

   Figure 4 reports the total unmet penicillin demand in Gaborone over 200 independent simulation rounds

for the three policies. As expected, the deterministic nominal model yields zero unmet demand because the

Negative Binomial demand, both the static robust and ARO-ADR policies exhibit substantial variability

trial therefore demonstrates that adjustable robustness can deliver incremental resilience benefits on top of

a static robust baseline, even in a simplified single-drug, single-district setting. Note, however, that the high

unmet demand indicated by the simulation could necessitate either further refinement of the model or simply

reflect a consequence of the variability of the draws from the negative binomial distribution.



6     Roadmap

As the following four items are all relatively nontrivial tasks that may require a significant amount of data

wrangling and troubleshooting, the goal is to get as much done as possible before the next milestone is due.


    • Model refinement. Continue to refine the model, developing ways to visualize the optimization

      results in interesting ways (including map-labeled figures), and find other ways to reduce unmet demand

      as much as possible.

    • Full-scale deterministic model. Scale the current implementation from the Gaborone case study

      to the full national CMS-DHMT-facility network, finalize remaining data cleaning, and verify that the

      deterministic formulation behaves correctly at national scope. It may be prudent to take this step

      district-by-district, then accumulate the results carefully.

    • Full-scale stochastic model (with affine decision updates). Also scale the current implementa-

      tion from the Gaborone case study to the full national CMS-DHMT-facility network, finalize remaining

      data cleaning, and verify that the stochastic formulation behaves correctly at national scope.

    • Incorporation of epidemiological uncertainty. Extend the demand model to include spatio-

      temporal epidemiological dynamics (e.g., SEIR-based shocks or seasonal variation). Determine how

      these disease-driven fluctuations should enter the optimization model and how they affect robustness

      requirements. Include any important derivations for this model addition. If available, run simulations,

      but since the theoretical implementation may take a while, simulations can be left for the period

      between the spring midterm report and final thesis submission.

    • Expiration and DALY-based penalties. Introduce antimicrobial shelf-life into the inventory prop-

      agation rules and evaluate how expiration costs change optimal holding and replenishment decisions.
